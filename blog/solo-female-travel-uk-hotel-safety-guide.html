What to Look For When Booking a Safe Hotel

Not all "4-star hotels" are equal when it comes to security. These are the features that actually matter for solo female travellers ‚Äî things our community consistently mentions in their safety reviews.

1. 24-Hour Staffed Reception

This is non-negotiable. A hotel with a real person at the desk around the clock means you always have someone to call if something feels wrong ‚Äî whether that's at 2am or 6am. Budget hotels often use automated check-in systems that leave you completely alone after hours. Always check before booking.

2. Keycard-Only Floor and Room Access

The best safe hotels use keycard access not just for your room, but for the lifts and corridors on your floor. This prevents non-guests from wandering into your corridor. It's a small feature that makes a big psychological difference when you're travelling alone.

3. In-Room Safe and Secure Door Locks

An in-room safe is important for valuables. Equally important: a door that can be secured from the inside with a chain or secondary bolt, not just the main lock. This is standard in good hotels but surprisingly absent in some budget options.

4. Location in a Low-Crime Postcode

A hotel's safety score on our platform reflects the actual recorded crime rate in its postcode ‚Äî not just the hotel's own marketing. A hotel can have excellent internal security but be located on a street with high street crime. Our hotel search tool shows both ‚Äî hotel security features and neighbourhood crime data ‚Äî so you can make a fully informed choice.

5. Well-Lit Entrance and Surroundings

Arriving late at night? The route from the taxi or Tube to your hotel entrance matters. Our community reviews consistently flag hotels where the entrance is poorly lit or set back from a main road. Look for reviews that specifically mention night-time arrivals.

Red Flags That Should Make You Rethink

‚ö†Ô∏è Watch out for these warning signs when researching hotels as a solo female traveller. They don't necessarily mean the hotel is unsafe, but they warrant more investigation before you book.

  • No mention of 24-hour reception on the hotel website ‚Äî always call to confirm if unclear
  • Reviews mentioning "thin walls" ‚Äî this can mean you'll hear (and be heard by) other guests
  • Ground floor rooms offered as standard ‚Äî always request a higher floor, ideally not accessible from outside
  • No in-room safe in a hotel where you'll be carrying valuable equipment or documents
  • Location near a major transport hub without further research ‚Äî stations like Paddington and Victoria attract petty crime in their immediate surroundings
  • Very cheap prices in central London ‚Äî if it seems too good to be true, the postcode or the security might explain why
  • Reviews from other solo female travellers mentioning discomfort ‚Äî this community always tells the truth in our reviews

What to Do On Arrival at Your Hotel

Good habits at check-in can make a real difference. These tips come directly from the solo female travel community on SafeHotels.ai.

At the Front Desk

When collecting your key, don't let staff announce your room number out loud. Ask them to write it down instead. It sounds obvious, but it's a step that's easy to overlook. Also ask which entrance is most secure for late returns and whether you can access it via keycard after midnight.

Choosing Your Room

Request a room between floors 2 and 6 ‚Äî high enough to be inaccessible from outside, but low enough to evacuate easily in an emergency. Avoid rooms directly next to stairwells or service lifts, which tend to have more foot traffic.

Securing Your Room

Use every lock available ‚Äî the main lock, chain, and any secondary bolt. Some solo travellers use a portable door alarm (around ¬£10 on Amazon) which attaches to the door handle and sounds if anyone attempts to enter. It's a small investment that provides real peace of mind.

‚úÖ Quick tip: Save your hotel's address and phone number in your phone before you arrive, along with the local police non-emergency number (101 in the UK) and nearest A&E. You hopefully won't need them, but having them ready costs nothing.
