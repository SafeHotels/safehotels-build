1

Check the Postcode, Not Just the Hotel Name

A hotel's star rating tells you about its facilities. Its postcode tells you about the neighbourhood you'll actually be walking around at night. These are two very different things. "Central London" or "Manchester City Centre" can mean anything ‚Äî the postcode is what matters. Our hotel search tool shows live crime data for every hotel's specific postcode before you book.

üí° Pro tip: Use Google Street View on your hotel's address at night mode to see what the surrounding streets actually look like after dark.
2

Request the Right Floor

Ask for a room between the 2nd and 6th floor. Ground floor rooms can be accessed from outside if windows or doors aren't perfectly secured ‚Äî and in older UK hotels, security on ground floor rooms can be variable. Above the 6th floor, you're safer from intruders but further from fire exits in an emergency. Floors 2‚Äì6 is the sweet spot.

üí° Pro tip: Also avoid rooms directly next to stairwells, ice machines, or service lifts ‚Äî these corridors get more foot traffic at all hours.
3

Never Say Your Room Number Out Loud

When the receptionist hands you your key, if they say your room number loudly in a public space, politely ask them to write it down instead. It's a small thing but means any bystander in the lobby doesn't know which room you're in. Good hotels do this automatically ‚Äî it's a sign of good security awareness.

4

Use Every Lock Available

Every door lock, chain, and secondary bolt should be used ‚Äî every single night. Don't assume the main lock is enough. A door chain means that even if someone has a key to your room (whether that's a maintenance issue or something more concerning), they cannot simply walk in while you're there.

üí° Pro tip: A portable door alarm (about ¬£10 online) attaches to the door handle and sounds if anyone attempts to enter. Brilliant for solo travellers in unfamiliar places.

Always use the in-room safe for passports, cash, and devices ‚Äî even for short trips out

5

Use the In-Room Safe ‚Äî Every Time

If your hotel has an in-room safe, use it every time you leave the room ‚Äî even for a quick trip to breakfast. Store your passport, spare cash, a backup bank card, and any devices you're not carrying. The few seconds this takes is the best insurance you can get against opportunistic theft during housekeeping.

üí° Pro tip: Change the safe's default PIN on arrival. Some hotels have master codes that all staff know.
6

Find the Emergency Exits on Arrival

This is one of the most skipped but genuinely important tips. When you arrive in your room, take two minutes to walk to the nearest emergency exit and check it opens correctly. In a real emergency ‚Äî fire, for example ‚Äî you want to know exactly where to go without thinking. Most UK hotels are very well maintained, but it only takes one locked exit in a panic to create a serious problem.

7

Be Smart With the Do Not Disturb Sign

The Do Not Disturb sign is useful, but be thoughtful about it. If you're a solo traveller leaving for the day, having DND on your door signals that the room is unoccupied and no one should notice anything amiss. Some solo travellers leave the TV on a low volume and remove the DND sign to make the room sound occupied. It's a small deterrent that costs nothing.

8

Check Whether Your Floor Has Keycard Access

The best safe hotels require a keycard to access the lifts for guest floors ‚Äî not just to open your room door. This means only registered guests and staff can get to your corridor. If you're concerned about safety, ask at check-in whether floor access requires a keycard. If it doesn't, it's worth bearing in mind when securing your room.

9

Know What Your Hotel Can Do for You at 3am

Before you need it, find out what your hotel offers around the clock. Can they arrange a licensed taxi? Is there security staff on site overnight? Can they hold your bags securely if your train is early? Hotels with 24-hour staffed reception ‚Äî not just a phone line ‚Äî are the ones that can actually help you when something goes wrong at an inconvenient hour. This is why we flag 24hr reception as a core safety feature across all our hotel listings.

10

Trust Your Instincts ‚Äî and Know What to Do If Something Feels Wrong

If something feels off ‚Äî whether that's an interaction at the desk, a fellow guest who's made you uncomfortable, or a room that doesn't feel secure ‚Äî act on it. Ask to change rooms. Speak to a manager. Call 999 in an emergency or 101 for police non-emergency in the UK. You are always entitled to feel safe where you're sleeping, and good hotels take these concerns seriously.
