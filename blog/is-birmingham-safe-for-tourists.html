Birmingham is one of the UK's most exciting cities — and one of the most misunderstood when it comes to safety. The UK's second-largest city has undergone a remarkable transformation over the past decade, with world-class restaurants, the revitalised canal network, Brindleyplace, and a vibrant cultural scene. But like any major city, where you stay matters enormously.

So — is Birmingham safe for tourists? The honest answer is yes, if you stay in the right areas. The difference between a relaxed Birmingham trip and a stressful one often comes down to a single postcode. Here's what the data actually shows.

Our Verdict: Generally Safe for Tourists. Birmingham's main tourist areas — the City Centre, Jewellery Quarter, Brindleyplace, and Edgbaston — are all considered safe for visitors. Crime in these areas is comparable to other major UK cities. Like any large city, some outer areas warrant more caution, but as a tourist staying centrally, you are very unlikely to encounter serious problems.

Birmingham Safety: The Big Picture

Birmingham city skyline — the UK's second largest city and a safe destination for tourists who stay in the right areas

Birmingham is a city of contrasts. The West Midlands as a whole has higher crime rates than the national average, but this is heavily concentrated in specific residential areas that tourists rarely visit. The city centre and popular tourist districts tell a very different story.

The city hosted the 2022 Commonwealth Games without significant safety incidents, welcoming millions of visitors from around the world. Areas to Approach with Caution

Every major city has areas that are less suitable for tourists, and Birmingham is no different. The following areas have higher recorded crime rates and few tourist attractions — there's simply no reason to stay in these parts of the city when safer central options exist at similar or lower prices.

⚠ Higher Crime Areas — Not Recommended for Tourists

Handsworth, Lozells, and Newtown are residential areas with significantly higher crime rates than the city centre. Small Heath and Bordesley Green similarly score lower on our safety index. None of these areas contain tourist attractions and our data confirms they have the lowest safety scores in our Birmingham database. Safer alternatives exist at comparable prices.

The simple rule: stay central. Any hotel in Edgbaston, the Jewellery Quarter, Brindleyplace, or the B1/B2 City Centre postcodes will give you an excellent experience. Our hotel search tool shows you the safety score for every Birmingham hotel's postcode so you can compare instantly before booking.

Practical Safety Tips for Visiting Birmingham

🚆
New Street Station Is Safe

New Street is busy, well-staffed, and very safe — but keep bags close in crowded periods like Christmas and sales. The Grand Central shopping centre above it is excellent during the day.

🚃
Use the West Midlands Metro

Birmingham's tram network is reliable, well-lit, and runs late. A safe way to move between Centenary Square, Brindleyplace, and the Jewellery Quarter — preferable to unfamiliar streets at night.

📍
Check the Postcode First

"Birmingham City Centre" covers a large area with varying safety levels. Enter any hotel's postcode into our search tool to see live crime data for that exact area before booking.

🚕
Use Licensed Taxis at Night

Taxis and Uber are reliable and well-regulated in Birmingham. Always use licensed cabs or the app, especially when travelling at night or arriving late by train from London.

Final Verdict

Birmingham is safe for tourists who stay in the right areas. The city's main attractions — the Bullring, the canals, the Jewellery Quarter, Edgbaston, and the cultural quarter — are all in areas with good safety records. The city has invested heavily in its tourist infrastructure and the experience for visitors has improved enormously in recent years.
